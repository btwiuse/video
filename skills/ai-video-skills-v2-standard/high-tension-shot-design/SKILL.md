# High Tension Shot Design

## Core Role

Increase visual tension while preserving readability. Tension comes from pressure, imbalance, contrast, timing, blocked escape, scale, proximity, or irreversible change—not from simply adding chaos.

## Tension Mechanisms

Choose one dominant mechanism per beat:

```text
proximity: subject and threat are too close
scale: subject is tiny against a huge force
imbalance: body, camera, or composition is off-axis
deadline: action must happen before impact
blocked escape: exits are closed or narrowing
conceal/reveal: key information is partially hidden
compression: frame, crowd, architecture, or enemy squeezes the subject
contrast: stillness vs sudden motion, silence vs impact, light vs dark
irreversibility: object breaks, door closes, weapon locks, fall begins
```

## Shot-Level Tools

### Composition

- Put threat on a strong diagonal path toward the subject.
- Use foreground obstruction to create pressure.
- Leave negative space where danger is about to enter.
- Crop slightly tighter when the character has fewer options.
- Use low angle for overpowering scale; high angle for vulnerability.

### Motion

- Start with a readable still or slow moment, then accelerate.
- Let the camera lag slightly behind a fast action to create pursuit pressure.
- Use a push-in when decision or threat becomes unavoidable.
- Use a pull-back to reveal scale or isolation.

### Light and Color

- Use controlled contrast, not random darkness.
- Put the eye on the brightest or sharpest meaningful area.
- Give danger a clear shape, color, or silhouette.
- Do not let effects destroy subject readability.

## Tension Prompt Pattern

```text
Tension design: [dominant mechanism]. The frame emphasizes [pressure source] by [composition/camera/light]. The subject has [limited option], and the shot peaks when [irreversible change/action] happens. Keep the main silhouette readable.
```

## Action Use

- Tension before impact: show distance closing, foot slipping, weapon nearing, shield cracking.
- Tension during impact: use readable contact and reaction, not only blur.
- Tension after impact: hold the damage, silence, stagger, or new power relation.

## Character PV Use

Make the character feel powerful through control, not clutter:

```text
calm pose against moving danger
clean silhouette with threat behind or below
single controlled gesture causing large consequence
final hold after the world reacts
```

## Common Fixes

- **Scene feels flat**: add one dominant tension mechanism.
- **Too chaotic**: remove extra effects and strengthen silhouette.
- **No danger**: show closing distance, blocked exit, scale difference, or irreversible change.
- **No hero image**: design a final pose with clean shape and one consequence.
- **Too much darkness**: keep key face/body/weapon edge readable.

## Negative Constraints

```text
no chaos for chaos, no random dutch angle, no unreadable darkness, no effects covering the subject, no tension mechanism conflict, no camera shake hiding the peak, no final frame without readable silhouette
```
